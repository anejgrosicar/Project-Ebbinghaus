Project Ebbinghaus is an online tool designed to help you not forget things worth remembering - inspired by studies of an experimental psychologist Herman Ebbinghaus, it relies on his findings about spacing effect and the forgetting curve; the more times we're exposed to a material, the higher the likelihood that we will remember it.

Hermann Ebbinghaus was a German psychologist and one of the leading pioneers of experimental psychology. He is most known for conducting experimental research on memory and processes of learning and forgetting. He's the discoverer of the forgetting curve and spacing effect, also known as spaced repetition.

Unfortunately, after being exposed to new information, humans are far more prone to forgetting it rather soon, as opposed to memorizing it - this is what Professor Ebbinghaus called the forgetting curve. To this day it is supported that memorization loss is exponential and that the sharpest decline happens quickly after initial learning and then tapers over time.

The Forgetting Curve: Memory's Natural Decline

However, as Ebbinghaus's research suggests, how we choose to learn new information plays a significant role in how much we end up remembering:

  1. Spacing effect: New information we learn should be conducted through increasingly spaced out intervals of study sessions (hence "spacing effect") - by forcing our brain to relearn information until perfect recall multiple times, we can reach near perfect long term retention of material we might have otherwise forgotten.
  2. Sufficiently long learning sessions: generally, sufficiently long learning sessions also improved retention, as they allowed for more repetition and contemplation on the material; one effective technique is called active recall.
